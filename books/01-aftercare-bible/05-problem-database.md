# Allergic Reaction

**Data Source**

`data/problems/allergic-reaction.yaml`

**Research**

`research/problems/allergic-reaction_research.md`

## Summary

Redness, itching, or rash around a piercing may sometimes reflect contact sensitivity to jewelry materials or aftercare products rather than normal healing.

## Risk Level

`moderate-concern`

## Key Recommended Actions

- Contact your piercer promptly for an in-person assessment of jewelry material and fit
- Discontinue suspected irritating products and switch to gentle sterile saline rinsing unless a clinician directs otherwise
- Do not remove jewelry on your own without professional guidance during early healing
- Ask your piercer about appropriate initial jewelry materials such as implant-grade titanium if sensitivity is suspected

## Actions to Avoid

- Assuming all redness means infection or that all itching is normal without assessment
- Switching to random jewelry materials without piercer guidance during healing
- Applying unprescribed topical steroids, antibiotic creams, or essential oils

## When to Seek Professional Help

- Rash spreads over a large area or appears far from the piercing site
- Itching or discomfort becomes severe or disrupts sleep and daily activity
- Swelling, warmth, or thick colored discharge develops alongside the rash
- You develop fever, dizziness, or difficulty breathing

# Bleeding

**Data Source**

`data/problems/bleeding.yaml`

**Research**

`research/problems/bleeding_research.md`

## Summary

Minor bleeding or spotting is common with new piercings and may also occur after trauma — persistent or heavy bleeding warrants professional review.

## Risk Level

`normal`

## Key Recommended Actions

- Apply gentle pressure with a clean gauze or paper product if active bleeding occurs
- Clean around the site with sterile saline once bleeding slows, without forcing crust off
- Be careful when brushing hair, dressing, or sleeping near the piercing
- Keep hands clean and minimize unnecessary contact with the site

## Actions to Avoid

- Pulling or picking dried blood crust off forcefully
- Removing jewelry during active bleeding without professional guidance
- Using tissue that sheds fibers against an actively bleeding piercing

## When to Seek Professional Help

- Bleeding is heavy, continuous, or does not slow with gentle pressure
- Bleeding recurs daily after the first week without clear trauma
- You feel dizzy, weak, or unwell alongside significant bleeding
- Bleeding occurs with spreading redness, severe pain, or thick colored discharge

# Crusting

**Data Source**

`data/problems/crusting.yaml`

**Research**

`research/problems/crusting_research.md`

## Summary

Dried fluid around a healing piercing is a common part of normal healing and is usually managed with gentle saline rinsing rather than picking or scraping.

## Risk Level

`normal`

## Key Recommended Actions

- Wash hands before touching the piercing or cleaning crust
- Spray or rinse gently with sterile saline wound wash during aftercare
- Use clean disposable gauze or cotton swabs to remove softened crust
- Pat the area dry with clean disposable paper products after cleaning

## Actions to Avoid

- Picking, scraping, or forcibly removing dry crust with fingernails or tools
- Twisting or rotating jewelry aggressively to break crust free
- Using alcohol, hydrogen peroxide, or harsh antiseptics to dissolve crust

## When to Seek Professional Help

- Crust becomes copious, green, gray, or foul-smelling
- Crusting is accompanied by spreading redness, warmth, or increasing pain
- Discharge beneath the crust is thick and yellow or green
- You develop fever or feel generally unwell

# Delayed Healing

**Data Source**

`data/problems/delayed-healing.yaml`

**Research**

`research/problems/delayed-healing_research.md`

## Summary

Healing timelines vary by piercing type, anatomy, and aftercare, but progress that remains unusually slow or stalled beyond typical expectations may warrant review by a qualified piercer. This concern is framed without diagnosing underlying medical conditions.

## Risk Level

`moderate-concern`

## Key Recommended Actions

- compare current symptoms with the healing timeline your piercer provided for this specific piercing
- book an in-person check-up so a qualified piercer can assess jewelry fit, placement, and aftercare
- simplify aftercare to brief sterile saline rinses and minimal handling unless directed otherwise
- note recent trauma, product changes, illness, or lifestyle shifts that may correlate with slower progress

## Actions to Avoid

- repeatedly changing jewelry or aftercare products without professional assessment
- assuming slow healing is always normal for every piercing type
- increasing harsh cleaning or unverified home remedies to force healing

## When to Seek Professional Help

- healing appears stalled beyond the timeframe your piercer gave for meaningful improvement
- progressive tightening, embedding, or visible migration of jewelry
- spreading redness, warmth, or swelling accompanied by worsening pain
- thick, yellow, green, or foul-smelling discharge

# Dryness

**Data Source**

`data/problems/dryness.yaml`

**Research**

`research/problems/dryness_research.md`

## Summary

Over-drying around a healing piercing can cause tightness, flaking, and discomfort. Gentle saline care and avoiding harsh products may help restore balance.

## Risk Level

`mild-irritation`

## Key Recommended Actions

- Reduce cleaning frequency to the range your piercer recommends, commonly one to three saline rinses daily for body piercings
- Use sterile saline labeled for wound wash with 0.9% sodium chloride as the primary ingredient
- Pat the area dry gently with clean disposable paper products after rinsing
- Rinse away any mild soap completely if your piercer suggests brief shower cleaning

## Actions to Avoid

- Mixing overly strong homemade salt solutions
- Applying alcohol, hydrogen peroxide, or undiluted essential oils to dry the site
- Scrubbing or picking at flaky skin around the jewelry

## When to Seek Professional Help

- Skin becomes deeply cracked, bleeding, or increasingly painful
- Redness spreads beyond the immediate piercing area or appears with warmth and swelling
- A widespread itchy rash develops that may suggest product sensitivity or allergy
- Dryness persists for weeks despite gentler aftercare adjustments

# Embedded Jewelry

**Data Source**

`data/problems/embedded-jewelry.yaml`

**Research**

`research/problems/embedded-jewelry_research.md`

## Summary

When jewelry ends sink into or become trapped by swelling skin, prompt piercer evaluation is needed — do not force jewelry or attempt removal without professional guidance.

## Risk Level

`urgent-professional-review`

## Key Recommended Actions

- Contact a qualified piercer promptly for an in-person assessment
- Do not force, twist, or pull jewelry through embedded tissue
- Leave jewelry in place unless a piercer performs a safe adjustment or change
- Reduce pressure and trauma to the area until evaluated

## Actions to Avoid

- Forcing jewelry forward or backward through skin that has grown over the ends
- Removing jewelry yourself when embedding is significant or infection is suspected
- Using pliers, needles, or non-sterile tools to free embedded ends at home

## When to Seek Professional Help

- Any jewelry end is partially or fully covered by skin
- You cannot clean around jewelry ends because they are sunken
- Swelling has improved but jewelry remains visibly too short
- Pain, heat, spreading redness, or thick discharge accompanies embedding

## Summary

Signs that may warrant professional evaluation when a piercing site looks or feels worse than expected — this record does not confirm infection.

## Risk Level

`urgent-professional-review`

## Key Recommended Actions

- Do not remove jewelry unless a healthcare provider or piercer specifically advises it
- Continue gentle saline cleaning unless a clinician directs otherwise

- You develop fever, chills, nausea, or feel systemically unwell
- Discharge is thick, colored, or foul-smelling
- Pain becomes severe or sudden

# Irritation Bump

**Data Source**

`data/problems/irritation-bump.yaml`

**Research**

`research/problems/irritation-bump_research.md`

## Summary

A small raised area near a piercing that often forms from mechanical irritation or aftercare habits rather than infection.

## Risk Level

`mild-irritation`

## Key Recommended Actions

- Leave jewelry in place unless a qualified piercer advises otherwise
- Identify and reduce sources of pressure, snagging, or friction
- Clean gently twice daily with sterile saline solution or a brief mild soap rinse in the shower
- Pat the area dry with clean disposable paper products

## Actions to Avoid

- Picking, popping, or squeezing the bump
- Applying tea tree oil, aspirin paste, or other home remedies without professional guidance
- Using alcohol, hydrogen peroxide, or antibiotic ointments unless directed by a clinician

## When to Seek Professional Help

- The bump grows quickly, becomes very painful, or does not improve after removing irritation sources
- You notice spreading redness, warmth, or swelling beyond the immediate piercing area
- Discharge becomes thick, yellow, green, or foul-smelling
- You develop fever or feel generally unwell

# Jewelry Quality Issue

**Data Source**

`data/problems/jewelry-quality-issue.yaml`

**Research**

`research/problems/jewelry-quality-issue_research.md`

## Summary

Initial or replacement jewelry that is poorly fitted, made from inappropriate materials, or mechanically substandard can contribute to irritation, allergic reactions, and healing complications. APP initial jewelry guidance favors implant-grade materials and appropriate sizing from a qualified piercer.

## Risk Level

`moderate-concern`

## Key Recommended Actions

- consult a qualified piercer to assess whether current jewelry meets APP initial jewelry guidance for material, fit, and finish
- consider implant-grade titanium, niobium, or appropriate karat gold only when confirmed suitable by a professional
- allow a piercer to measure for correct post length, diameter, and style rather than guessing sizing
- keep a simple sterile saline aftercare routine while jewelry is evaluated

## Actions to Avoid

- replacing jewelry at home with unverified online purchases during early healing
- assuming all irritation is an allergy without professional jewelry assessment
- wearing plated, surgical steel of unknown grade, acrylic, or fashion earrings in unhealed body piercings unless a piercer confirms suitability

## When to Seek Professional Help

- jewelry appears embedded, swallowed, or too tight to clean safely
- irritation persists after a professional jewelry change to appropriate material and fit
- widespread rash, swelling, or blistering develops around the piercing
- signs that may suggest infection such as fever or foul-smelling discharge appear

# Migration

**Data Source**

`data/problems/migration.yaml`

**Research**

`research/problems/migration_research.md`

## Summary

When jewelry shifts from its original placement and tissue narrows between the openings, professional evaluation is needed — migration can progress toward rejection if not addressed promptly.

## Risk Level

`urgent-professional-review`

## Key Recommended Actions

- Contact a qualified piercer promptly for an in-person assessment
- Leave jewelry in place unless a piercer advises a specific change or removal
- Stop sources of pressure, snagging, and friction on the site immediately
- Return to gentle sterile saline rinsing only if your piercer confirms it is appropriate

## Actions to Avoid

- Ignoring gradual jewelry movement or narrowing tissue between openings
- Removing jewelry yourself during suspected infection without professional guidance
- Applying harsh antiseptics, ointments, or unverified remedies to stop migration

## When to Seek Professional Help

- Jewelry position or angle has changed from the original placement
- Tissue between openings is progressively getting smaller or thinner
- Skin between openings is flaking, peeling, red, inflamed, or calloused-looking
- There is roughly one-quarter inch of tissue or less between openings

# Moisture Irritation

**Data Source**

`data/problems/moisture-irritation.yaml`

**Research**

`research/problems/moisture-irritation_research.md`

## Summary

Excess moisture around a healing piercing can soften tissue, trap debris, and contribute to irritation. Keeping the area clean and dry between routine care may help reduce discomfort.

## Risk Level

`mild-irritation`

## Key Recommended Actions

- rinse gently with sterile saline as directed by your piercer, then pat dry with clean disposable paper products or a low-lint gauze
- allow the area brief air exposure after cleaning when practical
- keep hair, clothing, and bedding from trapping moisture against the piercing
- shower with clean water and avoid letting shampoo, conditioner, or body wash pool around the site

## Actions to Avoid

- soaking the piercing repeatedly throughout the day
- applying thick ointments or occlusive products unless specifically advised by a qualified professional
- covering the piercing with non-breathable bandages for extended periods

## When to Seek Professional Help

- warmth, spreading redness, or swelling that worsens rather than fluctuates with care changes
- thick, yellow, green, or foul-smelling discharge
- fever or feeling systemically unwell
- pain that increases significantly or becomes difficult to manage

# Overcleaning

**Data Source**

`data/problems/overcleaning.yaml`

**Research**

`research/problems/overcleaning_research.md`

## Summary

Cleaning a healing piercing too often or too aggressively can strip protective crust, disrupt tissue, and worsen irritation. Professional aftercare guidance favors brief, gentle saline rinses rather than frequent scrubbing.

## Risk Level

`mild-irritation`

## Key Recommended Actions

- follow APP-style aftercare — brief sterile saline rinses once or twice daily, or as your piercer directs
- rinse gently without scrubbing; allow water and saline to flow across the site rather than forcing debris off
- pat dry with clean disposable paper products or low-lint gauze after rinsing
- leave the piercing alone between scheduled cleanings; minimize touching and jewelry movement

## Actions to Avoid

- cleaning the piercing many times per day without professional guidance
- using cotton balls or swabs that leave fibers around the jewelry
- rotating, twisting, or sliding jewelry to break up crust

## When to Seek Professional Help

- symptoms worsen despite returning to a gentle, APP-aligned aftercare routine
- spreading redness, increasing warmth, or swelling that does not improve with reduced handling
- thick, yellow, green, or foul-smelling discharge
- fever or systemic illness

# Pressure Irritation

**Data Source**

`data/problems/pressure-irritation.yaml`

**Research**

`research/problems/pressure-irritation_research.md`

## Summary

Constant pressure or friction against a healing piercing from clothing, gear, or body contact can keep tissue inflamed and delay comfortable healing.

## Risk Level

`mild-irritation`

## Key Recommended Actions

- Identify activities, garments, or gear that compress the piercing and adjust or pause them when possible
- Choose loose, breathable clothing that does not rub the jewelry during healing
- Use protective strategies your piercer recommends, such as a vented eye patch for navel piercings or sports bra support for nipples
- Clean gently with sterile saline and pat dry without aggressive scrubbing

## Actions to Avoid

- Continuing activities that clearly compress or twist the jewelry
- Tightening jewelry yourself to compensate for swelling without professional assessment
- Applying alcohol, hydrogen peroxide, or harsh antiseptics to inflamed tissue

## When to Seek Professional Help

- Redness, swelling, or pain worsens despite reducing pressure
- Jewelry angle changes noticeably or migration is suspected
- Skin between entry and exit becomes thin, hard, or calloused-looking
- Discharge becomes thick, yellow, green, or foul-smelling

# Rejection

**Data Source**

`data/problems/rejection.yaml`

**Research**

`research/problems/rejection_research.md`

## Summary

When the body pushes jewelry toward or through the skin surface, the piercing may be failing — prompt professional assessment is needed before scarring worsens.

## Risk Level

`urgent-professional-review`

## Key Recommended Actions

- See a qualified piercer as soon as you notice progressive surface movement
- Discuss whether jewelry material, size, or style change could stop progression
- Follow piercer guidance on retiring the piercing before tissue tears through
- Leave jewelry in place if infection is suspected until a professional advises otherwise

## Actions to Avoid

- Waiting until jewelry falls out on its own when rejection signs are clear
- Removing jewelry during suspected infection without professional coordination
- Covering the site with bandages or ointments in place of piercer assessment

## When to Seek Professional Help

- Tissue between openings is progressively thinner with inflamed or calloused skin
- One-quarter inch of tissue or less remains between openings
- Jewelry is visible through skin or only a thin filament of tissue remains
- You suspect the piercing is migrating past a viable depth

# Sleeping Irritation

**Data Source**

`data/problems/sleeping-irritation.yaml`

**Research**

`research/problems/sleeping-irritation_research.md`

## Summary

Pressure and friction from pillows, bedding, or sleep position can irritate healing piercings overnight and cause repeated morning tenderness or swelling.

## Risk Level

`mild-irritation`

## Key Recommended Actions

- Wash and change bedding and pillowcases regularly during healing
- Use the t-shirt pillow trick to create smooth, clean sleeping surfaces
- Place a travel pillow on your bed and rest your ear in the center opening to avoid direct pressure
- Try back sleeping or alternate sides to reduce repeated compression on one piercing

## Actions to Avoid

- Sleeping directly on a fresh cartilage piercing when avoidable
- Using dirty pillowcases or reusing unwashed bedding for long stretches
- Removing jewelry at night during early healing without professional guidance

## When to Seek Professional Help

- Morning pain or swelling worsens over several days despite sleep adjustments
- Jewelry angle changes noticeably or migration is suspected
- Redness spreads, discharge becomes thick or colored, or fever develops
- Embedded jewelry or skin growing over the post is noticed

# Snagging

**Data Source**

`data/problems/snagging.yaml`

**Research**

`research/problems/snagging_research.md`

## Summary

Catching or pulling healing jewelry on hair, clothing, or bedding can cause sudden pain, bleeding, and irritation that may slow healing if repeated.

## Risk Level

`mild-irritation`

## Key Recommended Actions

- Stay calm and avoid yanking jewelry out if it catches — gently untangle instead
- Leave jewelry in place unless a qualified piercer advises otherwise
- Clean gently with sterile saline and pat dry with clean disposable paper products
- Tie back or clip hair away from ear and facial piercings during healing

## Actions to Avoid

- Pulling jewelry out forcefully when it is caught
- Using cloth towels that can snag loops and barbells
- Hanging charms or heavy dangles before the piercing is fully healed

## When to Seek Professional Help

- Jewelry appears bent, embedded, or will not sit correctly after a snag
- Bleeding is heavy or does not stop with gentle pressure
- Pain, swelling, or redness worsens over 24 to 48 hours after trauma
- You notice thick, colored, or foul-smelling discharge after repeated irritation

# Swelling

**Data Source**

`data/problems/swelling.yaml`

**Research**

`research/problems/swelling_research.md`

## Summary

Puffiness around a new piercing is common in the first days of healing and may also signal irritation or a need for professional review when it worsens.

## Risk Level

`normal`

## Key Recommended Actions

- Keep jewelry in place unless a piercer advises a safe change for fit
- Apply a clean cold compress briefly if your piercer approves for your piercing type
- Sleep with the piercing elevated or avoid pressure on the site when possible
- Clean gently with sterile saline solution as directed by your piercer

## Actions to Avoid

- Forcing jewelry through tissue that feels too tight
- Taking jewelry out during significant swelling without professional guidance
- Applying ice directly to bare skin without a clean barrier

- Swelling worsens after the first few days instead of gradually improving
- Swelling spreads far beyond the piercing or affects facial symmetry noticeably
- Jewelry becomes embedded or skin grows over the ends
- Swelling is accompanied by spreading redness, warmth, thick discharge, or fever

# Undercleaning

**Data Source**

`data/problems/undercleaning.yaml`

**Research**

`research/problems/undercleaning_research.md`

## Summary

Insufficient or inconsistent gentle cleansing can allow buildup of normal healing discharge, sweat, and debris around a piercing. APP aftercare guidance supports brief daily sterile saline rinses to keep the site comfortably clean.

## Risk Level

`mild-irritation`

## Key Recommended Actions

- resume APP-style aftercare — brief sterile saline rinses once or twice daily, or as your piercer directs
- let saline flow across the site to soften crust; do not force or pick dried material off
- pat dry gently with clean disposable paper products or low-lint gauze after rinsing
- keep hair tied back and avoid makeup, lotion, or hair products contacting the piercing when possible

## Actions to Avoid

- ignoring crust buildup for many days out of fear of cleaning
- picking, scraping, or fingernail-removal of hardened crust
- using alcohol, hydrogen peroxide, or harsh soaps to compensate for missed cleanings

## When to Seek Professional Help

- thick, yellow, green, or foul-smelling discharge that persists after gentle saline care
- spreading redness, warmth, or swelling that worsens over time
- fever or feeling systemically unwell
- pain that increases significantly despite appropriate gentle cleaning
